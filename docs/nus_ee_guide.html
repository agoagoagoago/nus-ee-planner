How to read this guide

This report blends three very different kinds of information. They are tagged throughout so you always know what you are looking at.

Official NUS — taken directly from NUS / CDE / ECE pages and official curriculum PDFs, with a source number like [S5]. Treat these as authoritative (but always re-check against your own FFG/CourseReg checklist).

Interpretation — my reading, grouping, or restating of official material (for example, mapping a module to a "career pathway"). Reasonable, but not an official NUS classification.

Heuristic estimate — unofficial difficulty / workload / intensity ratings. These are my judgement calls to help you plan, not NUS ratings, not based on grade data, and not a promise about any individual module or instructor.

Read me first All difficulty, workload, math-intensity, programming-intensity, hardware-intensity and project-intensity numbers in Section F and the pathway "difficulty profiles" in Section G are heuristic estimates on a 1–5 scale, invented for this guide. NUS does not publish module difficulty ratings. Courses change instructors, syllabi, and assessment weightings year to year. Use these to sequence and balance your plan — never as the deciding factor for whether you can handle a module.

One vocabulary note: NUS now measures everything in Units (the old "Modular Credits / MC" term means the same thing). Most modules are 4 units; a few are 2 units; the capstone and the industrial attachment are larger. "Course" and "module" are used interchangeably.

A

Executive summary

The NUS Electrical Engineering degree is a 160-unit, four-year honours programme that front-loads a broad common foundation and a heavy engineering core, then hands you an unusually large amount of free space to specialise.

From the AY2025/26 cohort, EE and Computer Engineering share one College of Design & Engineering (CDE) curriculum structure built for "choice, depth and flexibility."[S1] You read 160 units (≈40 courses): 40 units of Common Curriculum, 80 units of Major Requirements, and 40 units of Unrestricted Electives (UE).[S1][S5]

The shape of the degree, in one breath:

  • Years 1–2 are mostly fixed. Maths, programming, an intro AI/ML course, circuits, signals, digital design, electromagnetics, energy systems and microcontrollers form a wide, compulsory base. There is very little choice here, and this is where the heaviest "weeder" modules live (electromagnetics, signals & systems, electronic circuits).
  • Specialisation effectively starts in Year 3. You pick one extended-core course from a concentration area, then two technical electives at the 4000-level, all inside the major. To go deeper you spend Unrestricted Electives on more of them — optionally bundling them into a named Specialisation (Robotics, Microelectronics & Quantum Materials, IoT, Space Technology, Sustainable Electric Transportation, Advanced Electronics, Industry 4.0) or a Minor / Second Major.[S1][S4]
  • A 10-unit Industrial Attachment is part of the major, not an optional extra, and a capstone (8 units, two semesters) — Design (EE4002D) or Research (EE4002R) — caps the final year.[S5][S6]
  • 40 units of UE is enormous — a full quarter of the degree is yours to direct. This is the single biggest lever you have over your employability and identity as a graduate.

What's genuinely new for AY2025/26

Compared with the AY2021/22–AY2024/25 structure,[S8] the Common Curriculum shrank from 60 to 40 units and the Major grew from 60 to 80 units. In practice that means the capstone moved into the major, a new EE3033 Systems Integration & Design Lab appeared, an extended-core elective became an explicit requirement, both EE2026 (Digital Design) and EE2028 (Microcontroller Programming) are now required (they used to be an either/or), and EE2022 grew from 3 to 4 units. Full comparison in Section J.

The three decisions that matter most

  1. Which concentration area(s) do you commit to? Your extended-core + two technical electives define your technical identity. Picking a coherent area (and ideally a Specialisation) reads far better to employers and grad schools than a scattered transcript.
  2. How do you spend 40 units of UE? Deeper EE (more electives / a Specialisation), a complementary Minor or Second Major (Computing and AI are popular and high-value for EE students), or breadth. There is no wrong answer, but there is an un-planned answer — drifting through random modules.
  3. Design capstone or research capstone, and when's your internship? These shape whether your final year points toward industry or postgraduate study.
Bottom line The compulsory core is fixed and demanding; your leverage is almost entirely in how you fill the electives and UE. Plan the depth (a coherent area) and the breadth (a deliberate use of UE) early, because most of the interesting electives sit at 4000-level with real prerequisite chains running back to Year 2.

D

Technical-elective area map

EE technical electives are organised into concentration areas. You pick your one extended-core elective and your two technical electives from these lists — and if you want a named specialisation on your transcript, you stack several courses from the same area.

The grouping below — which area each course sits in — is Official NUS, taken from the ECE Technical Electives page[S4] and cross-checked against the EE Courses list[S3]. The one-line description of what each area is about is my Interpretation to help you navigate. Each area shows its representative extended-core course (3000-level, the gateway you can use to satisfy the extended-core requirement) followed by its technical electives (mostly 4000-level).

How the three "specialise" slots work Your major gives you 1 extended-core elective (4u) + 2 technical electives (8u) = three free technical choices, plus the capstone (8u). You do not have to keep them in one area — but concentrating them is how you build genuine depth and how you qualify for a named NUS specialisation (typically ≥5 courses in the area, see Section G). Heuristic Most students who want a strong job story aim 3–5 of their free slots (technical electives + unrestricted electives + capstone topic) at a single area.

E

Plain-English curriculum map

Here is the whole degree in ordinary language: what you actually do each year, when you get to specialise, and what every category of elective is for.

The year-by-year layout below follows the official Official NUS recommended schedule for the AY2025/26 non-polytechnic intake[S5]. It is a recommended sequence, not a rigid one — many students shift courses by a semester around internships, exchange, or workload. Units per semester run roughly 20 / 20 / 20 / 20 / 22 / 18 / 20 / 20, totalling 160.

Year 1 — Foundations

  • Engineering maths: MA1511, MA1512, MA1508E
  • Programming: CS1010E
  • First EE labs: EE1111A, EE2111A (Principles & Practice I/II)
  • Design & making: EG1311, DTK1234
  • Data + project-finance literacy: GEA1000, PF1101A
  • Start chipping at unrestricted electives

Year 2 — The engineering core

  • The heavy EE core lands: EE2023 Signals & Systems, EE2012 Analytical Methods, EE2026 Digital Design, EE2027 Electronic Circuits, EE2028 Microcontrollers, EE2022 Energy Systems
  • Physics of fields: PC2020 Electromagnetics
  • The intro ML/AI choice: EE2211 or EE2213
  • Communication + Singapore-studies GE: ES2631, CDE2501, plus GEC/GEN pillars

Year 3 — Specialise + integrate + intern

  • Capstone-prep lab: EE3033 Systems Integration & Design
  • Professionalism: EG2401A
  • Specialisation begins: your 3xxxC extended-core elective + first technical electives
  • Industrial Attachment (10 units) — often over the long vacation or a dedicated semester

Year 4 — Depth + capstone

  • Capstone: EE4002D Design or EE4002R Research — 8 units across two consecutive semesters
  • Remaining technical electives (mostly 4xxx)
  • Remaining unrestricted electives — finish a minor/second major here if you're doing one

When does specialisation actually start?

Interpretation Mechanically, Year 3. Years 1–2 are almost entirely fixed: maths, programming, the ten EE core courses, electromagnetics, and the common-curriculum courses leave little room to choose. The first genuinely "you choose the topic" course is usually your extended-core elective in Year 3, followed by technical electives and your capstone topic. That said, the decision should start earlier — by mid-Year 2 you want a rough sense of which area excites you, because it influences which extended-core gateway you take and how you spend your unrestricted electives.

What are extended-core electives for?

Official NUS The major requires one extended-core elective (4u)[S6]. Interpretation Think of it as your "declare a direction" course: a 3000-level gateway (the 3xxxC courses in Section D) that opens up an area and is often a prerequisite-in-spirit for that area's 4000-level technical electives. Choosing EE3331C points you at control/robotics; EE3408C at analog ICs; EE3731C at signals/ML, and so on.

What are technical electives for?

Official NUS You take two technical electives (8u)[S6], chosen from the concentration areas in Section D. Interpretation This is where you build real depth. Two electives is the minimum the major demands — but because you also have 40 units of unrestricted electives, in practice you can take many more EE 4000-level courses if you want. Students aiming for a named specialisation or a strong technical interview story usually take 4–6 area courses by combining technical electives with unrestricted electives.

How can unrestricted electives be used?

Official NUS 40 units (about 10 courses) of unrestricted electives sit outside the major and can be almost anything in the university[S2]; they may be used toward a minor, a second major, or a specialisation. Interpretation This is the single biggest lever you control. Common strategic uses: (1) go deeper in your EE area by piling on more 4000-level EE courses; (2) add a credential — a Minor in Data Engineering, AI, or a second major in Computing; (3) broaden with business/entrepreneurship/language courses; (4) de-risk a heavy semester by parking an easier UE next to your hardest core courses. See Section H for how to spend them well.

How do the internship and capstone fit in?

Official NUS The Industrial Attachment is a 10-unit requirement inside the Engineering Core, and there are multiple ways to satisfy it — a full 10-unit attachment (EG3611A), a vacation route (CFG2101 + EG3612), or NUS Overseas Colleges (NOC), which can count for up to 20 units[S5][S11]. The capstone (EE4002D design or EE4002R research, 8u) runs across two consecutive semesters in the final year[S6]. Interpretation The internship usually happens in/around Year 3 and is the strongest single line on a fresh-grad résumé; the capstone is your chance to show you can take a real EE problem from spec to working artefact (design track) or to a research result (research track, the natural choice if you're aiming at graduate school).

NUS does not publish module difficulty ratings, and these are not based on grade distributions. They reflect common student experience and the nature of the material, and they shift with instructors and syllabus changes. Use them to sequence and balance your semesters — never as the sole reason to take or drop a module.

What the numbers mean, area by area

The maths block (MA1511 / MA1512 / MA1508E)

Individually each is moderate; the difficulty is that they arrive together in Year 1 and everything later leans on them. Heuristic Linear algebra (MA1508E) is the highest-leverage of the three — it underpins signals, control, and machine learning. Students who under-invest here feel it acutely in EE2023 and EE2012. Who finds it hard: anyone who treated A-level maths as something to memorise rather than manipulate.

The signals-and-maths spine (EE2012, EE2023, PC2020)

This is the conceptual heart of the degree and, for most students, the hardest stretch. EE2023 Signals & Systems is abstract and transform-heavy (Fourier/Laplace/z); EE2012 is probability and analytical methods; PC2020 Electromagnetics is regularly cited as the single toughest core course because it combines vector calculus with hard-to-visualise field physics. Heuristic Try hard not to take all three in one semester. Who finds it hard: students who are strong at coding but weak at continuous maths and proofs.

The hands-on core (EE1111A, EE2111A, EE2026, EE2027, EE2028, EE3033)

Lower on pure difficulty but high on workload, hardware and project axes — labs, reports, breadboards, FPGAs and microcontrollers eat time in chunks. EE3033 Systems Integration & Design is the capstone-prep pressure point: max project/hardware intensity. Heuristic Pair these with a lighter maths load, not with each other in a pile. Who finds it hard: students who procrastinate (lab work cannot be crammed) or dislike debugging physical systems.

Extended-core electives

All sit around difficulty 4 because they are the rigorous gateways into their areas. EE3331C (control) and EE3131C (comms) are maths-forward; EE3408C (analog IC) and EE3731C (signal analytics) add heavy lab/programming respectively. Heuristic Pick the one whose flavour of difficulty matches your strengths, because it sets the tone for that area's 4000-level courses.

Technical electives — the heavy hitters

Heuristic The most demanding technical electives cluster predictably: EE4302 Advanced Control and EE4104 Microwave Circuits (difficulty 5, maths 5); EE4205 Quantum Communication (difficulty 5); EE4308 Autonomous Robot Systems and EE4415 Integrated Digital Design (workload/project 5, programming 4–5); EE4212 Computer Vision (maths 5, programming 5). The gentler on-ramps are EE3105 Beyond Sky, EE4210 Network Protocols and EE4511 Renewable Generation. Who finds them hard: depends on axis — the control/RF/quantum courses punish weak maths; the robotics/vision/digital courses punish weak programming and poor time management on projects.

Capstone & internship

The capstone (EE4002D/R) is rated workload 5, project 5: not necessarily conceptually brutal, but it is sustained, self-directed and spans two semesters, so it competes with job-hunting and other 4000-level courses. The Industrial Attachment is low-difficulty but high-time during its block. Heuristic Keep the capstone semesters lighter on other heavy electives.

H

Four-year planning strategy

The official structure tells you what to take; this section is opinionated advice on how to sequence it well. Interpretation throughout — adapt to your own strengths and the live timetable.

Avoiding overloaded semesters

The recommended schedule already balances most semesters near 20 units, but the type of load matters more than the count. Use Section F to avoid stacking multiple difficulty-4/5 maths courses together. The classic trap is a single semester carrying EE2023, EE2012 and PC2020 at once — three maths-heavy cores. If your timetable allows, spread the spine across semesters and park a lighter unrestricted elective or GE next to your hardest course.

Balancing math-heavy and project-heavy modules

Think of each semester as a budget across two scarce resources: quiet analytical focus (maths/theory) and continuous hands-on time (labs/projects). Maths courses reward steady weekly problem-solving; project courses (EE2026, EE2028, EE3033, the capstone) consume large unpredictable blocks near deadlines. Heuristic Aim for at most one project-5 course per semester, and don't put your heaviest project course in the same term as two maths-5 courses.

Choosing between technical electives

Decide your primary area first, then choose electives that (a) build on your extended-core gateway, (b) avoid timetable clashes, and (c) mix one theory course with one build course so a single bad exam doesn't define your semester. Check real prerequisites on the live course pages before committing — this report marks most prerequisites “not found in source” (Section J). If you want a named specialisation, count backwards from its required course count and make sure your unrestricted electives cover the gap.

Planning for GPA

Heuristic Front-load achievable wins: keep Year-1 strong while the material is comparatively contained, because a solid early GPA buffers the harder Year-2 spine. Use unrestricted electives deliberately — a genuinely interesting, well-taught UE can both lift GPA and broaden you, but don't treat “easy” as the only criterion. NUS’s S/U-style options (confirm current rules with the registry) are best saved for a high-risk course in a heavy semester rather than spent early.

Planning for employability

Three levers dominate fresh-grad hiring: a relevant internship, a coherent elective story in one area, and a demonstrable project portfolio (capstone + course projects on GitHub). Pick a pathway from Section G and let your internship, capstone topic, technical electives, and a few unrestricted electives all point the same direction — depth reads far better to employers than a scattered transcript.

Planning for graduate school or research

If a research degree is the goal, choose the research capstone (EE4002R) over the design track, protect your GPA in the theory-heavy cores (signals, control, electromagnetics, probability), and seek a UROP or research assistantship early to get a reference letter and a publication-track project. Maths-5 electives in your area (e.g. EE4302, EE4212, EE4104) signal readiness for graduate coursework.

Planning for internships

The Industrial Attachment is built into the degree, and there are several official routes — a full attachment, a vacation route, or NOC for an entrepreneurial/overseas experience worth up to 20 units[S5][S11]. Interpretation Line your internship up with your intended area so it reinforces (not dilutes) your story, and start applying a semester or two ahead — the best placements recruit early. Treat the internship as the experiment that confirms or redirects your specialisation.

Using unrestricted electives strategically

Your 40 units of unrestricted electives are the biggest lever you control. The four common strategies: go deep (extra 4000-level EE in your area), credential up (a Minor in Data Engineering/AI or a second major in Computing), broaden (business, entrepreneurship, design, languages), or balance load (place a lighter UE beside your hardest core to protect that semester). Pick one dominant strategy rather than scattering all ten courses at random.

Gaps, assumptions & things to verify with NUS

Full transparency on what is solid, what is assumed, and what you must confirm against official NUS sources before relying on it.

Source-reliability note

The primary AY2025/26 EE curriculum-structure page[S10] was unreachable during research (the CDE site's bot-protection blocked automated fetching). Every structural figure in this guide was therefore corroborated from at least two other official NUS sources — the AY2025/26 recommended-schedule PDF[S5], the EE-with-iDP cohort-2025 structure[S6], the CDE common curriculum-structure page[S2], and the parallel ME structure[S7]. Still, open the primary page yourself to confirm the headline 40 / 80 / 40 split for your exact cohort.

What to verify

  • Prerequisites & co-requisites. Most are marked n/f (“not found in source”) in Section C because the structure pages don't list them course-by-course. Confirm each on the live NUS course catalogue / NUSMods before planning a chain. Official source needed
  • Exact per-bucket course allocation. The 60-unit EE Major Programme is reported as 10 core (40u) + 1 extended-core (4u) + 2 technical electives (8u) + capstone (8u). Confirm the precise counts and any “at least N from area X” rules against your cohort's FFG/CourseReg checklist.
  • Ungrouped courses. EE3703, EE4434 and EE4707 appear on the Courses page[S3] but not under any area on the Technical Electives page[S4]. Verify whether they currently count as technical electives and in which area.
  • Specialisation requirements. The 7 EE specialisation names are official, but the exact course lists and the “≥5 courses (≥2 compulsory)” rule used here is a general pattern — confirm the specific requirement for your cohort.
  • Industrial Attachment options. The full-attachment / vacation / NOC routes and their unit values[S5][S11] change periodically — confirm current codes and unit counts.
  • Extended-core list for Power (PES). PES's gateway is less prominent on the electives page because EE2022 already sits in the core; confirm which course officially satisfies the extended-core requirement for a power focus.
  • Unit values. Most electives are assumed to be 4 units (with EE4032 at 2u and the capstone/IA larger). A handful may differ — confirm on the catalogue.
The non-negotiable caveat All Difficulty / Workload / Math / Programming / Hardware / Project ratings in Sections F and G are unofficial heuristic estimates created for this guide. NUS does not publish module difficulty ratings; these are not derived from grade data and will not match every student's experience or every instructor's offering. Use them to plan balance and sequence — never as the sole basis for a course decision, and never represent them as official NUS figures.
